Getting Started with OSINT: A Beginner's Guide

Open Source Intelligence (OSINT) is the practice of collecting and analyzing publicly available information to produce actionable intelligence. In CTF competitions, OSINT challenges test your ability to find information that's hiding in plain sight, no hacking required, just clever searching.

💡 Good news: OSINT is one of the most beginner-friendly CTF categories. You don't need to write any code, just know where to look.
⚠️ Ethics first: OSINT skills must only be used on yourself, with explicit permission, or in authorized CTF/research contexts. Never use these techniques to stalk or harm others.

What is OSINT?

OSINT draws from publicly accessible sources: websites, social media, public records, DNS data, image metadata, and more. The goal is to piece together information that people didn't realize was public.

In CTF competitions, OSINT challenges might ask you to find a person's location from a photo, identify a building from a street view screenshot, or track down an account across platforms using a username.

Essential OSINT Tools

🔍
Google Dorking
Advanced Google search operators to find specific file types, exposed directories, login pages, and more. No extra software needed.
Example: site:gannon.edu filetype:pdf "confidential"
🌐
Shodan
The "search engine for the Internet of Things." Finds internet-connected devices, open ports, running services, and default credentials.
shodan.io ↗
📸
ExifTool
Extracts hidden metadata from images and files, GPS coordinates, camera model, timestamps, author info, and more.
Install: sudo apt install exiftool
👤
Sherlock
Searches 300+ social media platforms for a given username simultaneously. Great for tracking down accounts across the web.
Usage: python3 sherlock.py username
🗺️
Google Maps / Street View
For GeoINT challenges, identifying locations from photos by matching landmarks, street signs, shadows, or architecture styles.

Step-by-Step: Solving an OSINT CTF Challenge

Example Challenge

You're given a photo of a person standing in front of a building and asked: "What city was this taken in?"

Step 1: Extract Metadata

$ exiftool challenge.jpg
GPS Latitude  : 41° 50' 57.60" N
GPS Longitude : 80° 6' 57.60" W

If GPS data exists in the EXIF, you're done. Plug the coordinates into Google Maps.

Step 2: Reverse Image Search

If no metadata, drag the image into Google Images or Yandex Images (often better for buildings). Look for matching results that reveal the location.

Step 3: Identify Visual Clues

Look for:

  • Street signs, bus numbers, or license plates
  • Unique architectural features or landmarks
  • Language on storefronts or posters
  • Sun position to estimate time of day and hemisphere
  • Vegetation types for climate hints

Step 4: Cross-reference on Maps

Use Google Street View to walk around suspected locations until the view matches the photo.

Useful Google Dork Operators

site:example.com         , limit results to a domain
filetype:pdf             , find specific file types
inurl:admin              , find admin panels
intitle:"index of"       , find open directories
"confidential" site:.gov , find leaked docs on .gov sites
cache:example.com        , view Google's cached version

Practice Resources

  • PicoCTF, beginner CTF with many OSINT challenges
  • GeoGuessr, builds your visual location identification skills
  • Bellingcat Online Investigation Toolkit, professional OSINT resources
  • TryHackMe, has dedicated OSINT learning paths
OSINT is less about tools and more about mindset. Train yourself to ask: "What else is publicly available about this?", and you'll be surprised how much you can find.
